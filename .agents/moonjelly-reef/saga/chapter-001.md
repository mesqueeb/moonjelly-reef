# Chapter 1 — The Missing Note

The octopus named Vesper had a workshop that confounded everyone who visited. Shells sorted by radius lined one shelf. Below it, a tangle of coloured string that she insisted was sorted by feel. Somewhere in the middle of it all, pinned to a rock with a piece of coral, were her notes.

Or they were supposed to be.

She turned over a coil of kelp rope, checked beneath a stack of flat stones, and patted down every shelf with three of her eight arms at once. No note. She had been halfway through a very good idea — something about threading abalone chips with seagrass — and then she had set the note down for one moment, and now the moment had expanded into a crisis.

"Vesper," said a voice from the doorway.

The moonjelly Luma drifted in without quite being invited, which was her usual method. She glowed faintly even in the afternoon light, the kind of warm amber-pink that made the whole workshop look like late evening.

"I'm busy," Vesper said.

"You're turning everything upside down and calling it busy," Luma said pleasantly. She drifted sideways along the shelves, peering with what she probably thought was a subtle expression. "What did you lose?"

"A note. It had my next step on it. The whole plan."

Luma paused by a particular shelf — the one that held the smooth objects, the ones without edges. "Was it written on a flat white shell?"

Vesper went very still. "Where."

"The barreleye had it." Luma's glow brightened a fraction. "He picked it up from the current two hours ago. He thought it was a found thing. He's been using it to press seaweed."

Vesper said something that will not be repeated here, and then she was already halfway out the door.

The barreleye — who went by Pip, a name he'd acquired through absolutely no fault of his own — was crouched in his usual spot near the cold outflow at the north edge of the reef, peering at a thin ribbon of kelp from an angle that would have given anyone else a cramp. He looked up when Vesper arrived, tilting his huge barrel-eyes in her direction.

"Ah," he said.

"My note," Vesper said.

He produced it without argument. It was slightly damp and had a small pressed-seaweed mark in the corner, but otherwise intact. Vesper read it, exhaled through two of her arms, and tucked it safely beneath the edge of her arm-band.

"I thought it was decorative," Pip said, in honest explanation rather than apology.

"It was a plan," Vesper said. "Plans and decorations are different things."

Pip tilted his head the other way. "Are they always?"

Vesper looked at him for a long moment. Then she looked at the pressed-seaweed mark in the corner of her note, at the careful way he'd laid it under a smooth stone to keep it flat. She almost said something sharp. Instead she said: "Next time, bring found things to me first."

"Agreed," said Pip, and turned back to his kelp with evident relief.

Vesper returned to her workshop. Luma was still there, which was not a surprise. She had rearranged a small section of the string shelf and was looking rather pleased with herself.

"Don't," Vesper said.

"I improved it," Luma said.

"You moved it."

"Those are the same thing."

Vesper considered throwing a shell. She set the note back under its proper coral pin instead, and started again on the abalone idea. Luma settled near the ceiling, glowing softly, and for once did not say anything at all. Outside, the current hummed past. The workshop was warm. The plan, recovered and intact, turned out to be a good one.

At first light, the moonjelly drifted through the work ledge with a pale pulse under her bell, and the octopus looked up from a nest of string as if he had been expecting that exact glow. Near the tide pool, the crab clicked a tighter clasp onto a shell lantern, and for once nobody argued with the quiet sense that the reef had just found the right shape for the morning.
By midday, the tide pool had given up a small curve of polished shell exactly the size the lantern needed, and the crab held it up to the light with the offended look of someone who had been outsmarted by kindness. The octopus only hummed and slid the piece into place, while the moonjelly drifted closer as if she had always known the reef was leaving them notes.
When the current turned, a thin thread of sea grass surfaced beside the ledge, braided through with two more small fixes, each one chosen as neatly as a word in a sentence. The crab went very still, then pretended not to, while the octopus touched the braid once and smiled as if some patient hand in the water had finally answered back.
As the light softened, the diver found a coil of fine copper tucked beneath a stone at the tide pool edge, warm as if it had just been held. The crab huffed at the sight, but did not move to stop the moonjelly from carrying it to the ledge, where the octopus set it beside the lantern like the reef had simply decided what it wanted to become next.
At dusk, the octopus warmed the copper coil in one careful hand, and when he laid it against the lantern, something under the ledge answered with a soft bright click, as if the reef had found its own seam. The crab lifted one claw toward the glow instead of a complaint, and the moonjelly drifted past them both with the calm certainty that the next piece would come when it was ready.
At the edge of the tide pool, a key-shaped sliver of pearl surfaced, and when the octopus laid it beside the lantern, the soft click under the ledge answered from somewhere farther out in the bowl, as if the reef had made room for a door no one had seen yet. The crab did not complain; he only watched the darkening water, and the moonjelly drifted upward with a glow that felt less like an ending than an invitation.
